A note on what your data is showing
Your network is heaviest in Chicago, Memphis, and Atlanta. Sixty percent of completed discernments name mixed-status families as the population people keep returning to. Your Faithful Witnesses (110 people) are concentrated in those same metros, suggesting natural cohort formation around accompaniment work. Worth considering: a regional infrastructure investment in any of these three cities would compound across the formation already happening.

What this is

A coalition partner dashboard showing CCDA's view of the Faithful Witness Discernment Experience network attributed to them. Sample data shows realistic scale 12 weeks after a partner begins driving traffic: 487 people who came through their network, 421 currently in formation, 312 who have completed the full journey, 64 teams forming, 19 of those teams sparking new teams.

The privacy posture

Aggregate data only. Per the Partner Data Promise, partners see counts, distributions, and patterns. They never see individual names, individual responses, sense-of-calling text, journal entries, or whether anyone in their network is directly affected by the immigration system. The dashboard's privacy pill at the top makes this commitment visible. The minimum-count threshold of 10 is enforced on all sub-aggregates; metrics for very small networks would show "Insufficient data to display" rather than expose statistics that could re-identify individuals. (CCDA's scale doesn't trigger the threshold; smaller partners' dashboards would.)

The story this dashboard tells

For CCDA specifically, the dashboard tells a story their leadership can use in board reports, grant applications, and conversations with member churches. Twelve weeks. 487 people. 64 teams. 19 of those teams sparking new ones. Mixed-status families as the dominant focus. Chicago, Memphis, Atlanta as the centers of gravity. This is what CCDA's leadership has never had before: a real-time picture of formation happening across their network, in language a board can absorb in 30 seconds.

The funder story this dashboard tells

For a funder reviewing the platform, this dashboard demonstrates four things:

One — the platform is movement infrastructure. The same machinery that runs this dashboard for CCDA would run it for NaLEC, for World Relief, for Undivided, and (later) for any coalition organizing around any cause. Immigration is the proof of concept; the multi-tenant network dashboard is the asset.

Two — formation is happening. Three hundred and twelve completed discernments in twelve weeks across one network. Sixty-four teams. Multiplication starting to compound. The numbers are real (in this mockup, realistic; in production, true).

Three — the data is honest. The privacy commitments are visible, enforced architecturally, and named in the partnership agreement. The platform has earned the trust required to be in this position with participants and partners both.

Four — the partner value is concrete. CCDA gets reach, visibility, geographic insight, gifting and profile composition of their network, and a tool for publishing events that surface to people already in formation around their cause. This is what makes coalition partnership worth their time and what funds the ongoing partnership.

Architecture notes

Attribution mechanism. Every number on this dashboard is derived from participants whose root inviter came through CCDA's ?utm_source=ccda link. This is the entire technical machinery: clean slug discipline at first touch, then walk the invite chain to attribute downstream participants. The Partner Slug Convention document specifies how this works.

Multi-tenant. The same dashboard renders for any partner with the same template, just different attribution. Adding a new partner is an entry in the slug registry and an authentication grant, not new code per partner.

Aggregate-only enforcement. The backend queries that populate this dashboard are different from the queries that populate Victor's coalition-level dashboard, and are different from the queries that populate a team leader's view. The same database; different access patterns; enforced at the API layer with row-level security as a defense-in-depth backstop.

What's deliberately NOT here

Individual team leader names. The dashboard does not surface "Sarah Martinez is leading a successful team in Cincinnati" because that would identify a specific participant. Teams that opt into network visibility could appear in a future "Featured teams" surface; until then, all team-level reporting is by counts only.

Themes from open-text responses. The Data Promise commits to surfacing aggregated themes (≥10 participants sharing a theme), but the clustering implementation is deferred. When built, themes will appear in a dedicated panel here.

Direct contact mechanism. The Data Promise allows for opt-in partner-to-participant contact via the platform. The UI for that mechanism is not yet built. When built, it would surface as a small "Reach out" affordance for participants who have specifically opted in to hearing from this partner.

Cross-network views. Each partner sees their own network only. Coalition-level aggregate views (across CCDA + NaLEC + World Relief + Undivided combined) are accessible only to Victor and platform staff, never to individual partners.
